This is an editorial specification, not the diagram itself. It contains no pixel coordinates, no SVG, no rendering code, and no invented architecture. Every statement below maps back to a sentence already present in the frozen AUTHORIZATION.md Figure B section, or to the conceptual model already reviewed and approved in Figure-B-blueprint.md. 01Purpose

Figure A taught a reader where authorization is enforced. This figure teaches something Figure A cannot: how an authorization decision is actually put together, conceptually, before it resolves. A reader who has already understood Figure A's three enforcement tiers should leave this figure understanding that a decision is not a single check — it is a small number of equally necessary conditions, all of which must hold at once, plus a small number of rules that can still override what those conditions alone would grant.

This figure exists because that structure cannot be read out of Figure A. Figure A shows where the dedicated authorization component sits in the enforcement pipeline. It says nothing about what that component — or the governance rules layered around it — actually reasons through. Figure B fills that gap, and only that gap.

02Engineering Question

How is an authorization decision progressively built, from identifying the actor to allowing a protected business operation?

This is the question frozen in AUTHORIZATION.md's Figure B section, verbatim in substance. It is not a question about execution order, request lifecycle, filters, endpoints, or resources — those belong to Figure A or to a different artefact entirely.

03Architectural Message

VookedMe builds every authorization decision through the same conceptual sequence, regardless of the operation involved: it starts from the role model, narrows through business membership, narrows again through the breadth of the actor's authorization scope, and finally passes through a small set of governance rules that can still override or restrict what scope alone would grant. A decision that clears every step is granted; failing any one of them denies it. Non-panel actors — Customer, Bot, System — never enter this sequence at all; they are authorized through an entirely separate mechanism, outside the role model altogether.

04Visual Philosophy

This figure explains HOW a decision is constructed — not WHERE it is enforced, and not WHEN it executes. It is a conceptual model, not an execution flow, not a request lifecycle, not a filter chain, not a CRUD matrix, not an RBAC table, not an endpoint map, and not a resource map. A reader should come away with a mental model of the reasoning, never a map of the code.
The composition represents progressive conceptual narrowing, not chronology. Chronology is secondary. Responsibility — in the sense of "what must still be true for this decision to hold" — is primary. Two decisions built through this same model might be evaluated by different services, in a different literal runtime order; what is constant is the set of conditions the decision depends on, narrowing conceptually from broad to specific, not the order any specific request happens to execute them in.
Authorization here is cumulative, not hierarchical, and not competitive. Figure A earned a single dominant anchor because one component genuinely concentrated most of the responsibility. Figure B has no such anchor, by design. Role Model, Business Membership, Authorization Scope, and Governance Rules are four dimensions that jointly contribute to one decision — a progressive conceptual narrowing from broad to specific, not a queue of steps. Removing any one of them makes the decision incomplete, not merely smaller. No node in this figure is permitted to look more important than the others, and no reading path through them may feel mandatory or ordered.

Connector Philosophy

  • Connectors communicate conceptual narrowing, never chronology. A connector never means "and then." It means "this constraint must also be satisfied."
  • Connectors never imply execution. Business Operation is not reached because a series of steps completed in turn; it is reached because every dimension it depends on remains valid at once.
  • Nodes teach; connectors only clarify which constraint attaches to which. The same rule established for Figure A holds here — a reader should be able to reconstruct the model from the nodes alone.
  • No connector may be styled with more visual weight than the node it touches. This applies equally to all nine connectors in this figure; none of them is permitted to look more consequential than another, matching the "no visual winner" rule that governs the nodes themselves.

05Conceptual Model

The model frozen in AUTHORIZATION.md's Figure B section, stated in full:

Role Model
↓
Business Membership
↓
Authorization Scope
↓
Governance Rules
↓
Business Operation

Access Denied is a shared outcome, reachable from more than one of these dimensions — not a fifth dimension reached only after the other four are exhausted. Customer, Bot, and System form an authorization model that is independent of this decision model entirely: they are intentionally outside the platform role hierarchy, and the figure must show them as such, not as a variant path through the same four dimensions.

Read as a sentence, not as a diagram: an authorization decision is granted when the caller's role is known, their business membership holds, their authorization scope covers the action, and no governance rule overrides that outcome — four conditions considered jointly, not consumed in turn. Any single failure among those four denies the decision immediately, without needing to evaluate the remaining dimensions.

06Node Taxonomy

Seven nodes. For each, this section explains why it exists as a node at all, why it is not folded into a neighbouring node, why it occupies its position, and — as important as what it represents — what it explicitly does not represent.

Role Model

Why it is a nodeIt is the question every decision must start from: which kind of actor is asking. No later node can be evaluated before this one is known.
Why not folded elsewhereIt cannot be folded into Business Membership, because two of the three non-panel actor categories never have a business membership question asked of them at all — the departure from the platform role hierarchy belongs to this dimension alone.
Why this positionThe entry dimension — every other dimension in the group presumes an actor category is already known.
RepresentsThe three flat platform roles (ADMIN, OWNER, EMPLOYEE), and the fact that three further actor categories exist entirely outside this role structure.
Does NOT representAuthentication or credential issuance. This node presumes identity is already known; establishing it is Security Architecture's concern, not this figure's.

Business Membership

Why it is a nodeTenant boundary is a distinct, independently-failing question from "how broad is this actor's standing." A caller can belong to no relevant business at all, which ends the decision before scope is ever asked.
Why not folded elsewhereIt is not part of Authorization Scope, because membership is a yes/no gate with no breadth of its own, while scope is graded (self, ownership, platform-wide). Merging the two would hide that a caller can fail outright before any grading question is even relevant.
Why this positionA dimension conceptually prior to Authorization Scope — it establishes tenant context, which the breadth-of-standing question then presumes, without being reached through it as a processing step.
RepresentsThe tenant-isolation boundary: does this caller belong to the business the operation targets.
Does NOT representWhich specific operation is being attempted, or which record is involved. It is a boundary check, not a permission check.

Authorization Scope

Why it is a nodeOnce membership holds, the model still needs to ask how far the caller's standing reaches — the same membership can carry very different authority depending on whether the caller is acting on their own behalf, holds leadership standing, or holds platform-wide authority.
Why not folded elsewhereIt is not part of Governance Rules, because scope answers "could this caller plausibly do this at all," while governance answers "is there a reason to still say no, even though scope was satisfied." Collapsing the two would hide that governance rules are exceptions layered on top of scope, not another instance of it.
Why this positionA dimension that presumes a business context to evaluate standing within (or, for platform-wide standing, presumes exemption from that context) — conceptually dependent on Business Membership, without being reached through it as a step.
RepresentsThe breadth of an actor's standing, held as three internal breadths within this single dimension — Self (acting on their own behalf), Leadership (ownership/leadership standing), and Platform-wide (authority independent of any business). These three remain internal content describing one dimension; they are not additional nodes.
Does NOT representAny single named check, method, or class. This node is the concept of "how broad," not an inventory of the mechanisms that decide it.

Governance Rules

Why it is a nodeSome real, documented restrictions in VookedMe cannot be expressed as "how broad is your standing" at all — they hold regardless of standing, or they narrow a decision for reasons scope has no vocabulary for.
Why not folded elsewhereIt is not part of Authorization Scope for the reason given above; it is not part of Business Operation either, because these rules are evaluated before the operation is reached, not as a property of the operation itself.
Why this positionThe dimension conceptually last in narrowing specificity — the one capable of overriding what the other three dimensions alone would already allow.
RepresentsA small set of override constraints, applied after Authorization Scope is already satisfied — not a further grading of who-may-act, but a check for standing reasons to still say no: barring self-approval of one's own request, reserving certain actions to a single role, respecting a record's own lifecycle state, and respecting a business's own configuration choices.
Does NOT representA general "misc rules" catch-all, and not a further RBAC layer alongside Authorization Scope. Every rule inside this node is individually named and traceable to AUTHORIZATION.md's Governance Rules section — nothing here is a placeholder for undocumented exceptions.

Business Operation

Why it is a nodeThe decision model needs a destination — the thing all four dimensions jointly exist to guard.
Why not folded elsewhereIt cannot be merged into Governance Rules: it is an outcome of the decision model, not a dimension within it. Conflating an outcome with a dimension would misstate what this node is.
Why this positionThe model's positive resolution — reachable only when every dimension holds simultaneously, not as a next stop after the last one finishes.
RepresentsThe granted, positive outcome: the protected business action the model exists to guard.
Does NOT representAny specific operation, aggregate, or endpoint. It is the concept of "the guarded thing," not an inventory of guarded things.
Naming reviewed, retained: "Business Operation" was reviewed against whether a clearer term exists for "the protected action after authorization." No better candidate was found that remains faithful to AUTHORIZATION.md's own language (which describes "the protected business operation" proceeding) while preserving the exact term Figure A already established for the same outcome. The name is unchanged.

Access Denied

Why it is a nodeAny of three dimensions can independently fail, and each failure needs a destination. Rather than three separate "denied" endings, one shared node teaches the idea once.
Why not folded elsewhereIt must not be positioned as a fifth dimension. Doing so would misstate it as something reached only after the other four are exhausted, when in fact it can be reached from any of three of them, immediately.
Why this positionOutside the dimension group entirely, reachable from three of its members rather than sitting inline among them.
RepresentsThe shared negative outcome: a decision denied because membership, scope, or a governance rule failed.
Does NOT representA fourth or fifth constraint, a processing stage, or a distinct rule of its own. It performs no reasoning — it is only ever a destination.

Non-Panel Actor Authorization

Why it is a nodeCustomer, Bot, and System are authorized at all — the figure would misrepresent the model if it simply omitted them.
Why not folded elsewhereIt must not be attached anywhere within the dimension group. None of the three actors passes through Business Membership, Authorization Scope, or Governance Rules — attaching this node to that group would falsely imply they do.
Why this positionReachable only from Role Model, and connected nowhere else — the departure happens at that one dimension and never rejoins the rest of the model.
RepresentsA single concept standing in for three actors: identity-matching (Customer), channel credential (Bot), and trusted-infrastructure status (System).
Does NOT representA variant or relaxed version of the role-based dimension group. It is a structurally separate mechanism, not a fourth actor category added to Role Model's three.

07Node Responsibilities

What each node is architecturally responsible for deciding, stated as a single question per node — the same discipline already used for the dedicated authorization component's own responsibilities table in AUTHORIZATION.md.

Authorization ScopeHow broad is this actor's standing — their own behalf, leadership, or platform-wide?

09Visual Hierarchy

Unlike Figure A: no dominant anchor, no oversized node, no visual winner. Hierarchy in this figure is expressed through spacing, alignment, negative space, and reading rhythm — never through node size.

TierMembersHow the tier is expressed
Primary (equal weight)Role Model, Business Membership, Authorization Scope, Governance RulesIdentical size, identical border weight, identical accent treatment. Equal, steady spacing between each. No node in this tier may be rendered larger, heavier, or more saturated than its neighbours, regardless of how much internal detail one node carries versus another.
Outcome (lighter)Business Operation, Access DeniedVisibly quieter than the primary tier — destinations, not decisions. Equal to each other.
Supporting (quietest)Non-Panel Actor AuthorizationThe lightest node on the canvas, positioned apart, reached by a single dashed line and nothing further.
A specific risk to guard against: Authorization Scope and Governance Rules each carry an internal sub-list (three items and four items respectively), which will naturally want to render taller than Role Model or Business Membership. This must not be allowed to read as "these two are bigger, therefore more important." The internal sub-list text must be set small enough, and the four primary-tier containers wide enough and consistent enough in their own right, that any height difference reads as "this one happens to hold a short list," never as a size-based importance signal.

10Layout Blueprint

Described relationally. No pixel coordinates, no embedded mockup, no SVG, no CSS positioning, and no prescribed artistic technique — only relative composition rules for a renderer to satisfy.

This is a decision model, not a pipeline. Role Model, Business Membership, Authorization Scope, and Governance Rules are four dimensions that jointly constitute one decision — not four steps that process a request in turn. A rendering that resembles a top-to-bottom flowchart, where each box waits for the previous one to finish, has misread this section. The composition must communicate "these four together decide," never "these four happen in turn."

Central conceptual axis

The four dimensions form one composed group — a single conceptual backbone the reader treats as one unit, not four independent boxes and not four ordered stages. Whatever concrete arrangement a renderer chooses for this group, no two of the four may be arranged in a way that implies one depends on the completion of another; each is a contributing condition to the same decision, considered together, not consumed in turn.

Surrounding reasoning layers

Business Operation and Access Denied sit outside this central group, as its two possible resolutions — not as further dimensions and not as additional stops along a line. Non-Panel Actor Authorization sits further outside still, connected only to Role Model, as a reasoning domain the central group never reaches at all. These surrounding layers frame the dimension group; they do not extend it.

Grouped decision dimensions

Authorization Scope and Governance Rules each hold further internal structure (three breadths; four rules), but this internal richness must read as depth within one dimension of the group, never as sub-stages a reader is meant to step through in order. The internal list inside each is detail a reader may consult, not a further layer of narrowing they must traverse.

Balanced composition

The four dimensions carry identical visual weight and must be composed so that no reading path through them feels mandatory or ordered. The composition should read as settled and balanced — the four dimensions co-existing as one layered conceptual composition — rather than directional. Business Operation and Access Denied are the only two elements that read as destinations; everything else reads as a dimension or a reasoning domain, considered jointly rather than traversed.

Negative space

Three deliberate zones of separation organise the canvas: clear, even space among the four dimensions themselves — no dimension crowds another, and none gains prominence through spacing that another lacks; a further zone of separation between the dimension group and its two outcomes, marking them as resolutions rather than further dimensions; and the widest zone of separation on the canvas between the dimension group and the non-panel reasoning domain, marking it as the most structurally distant relationship in the figure.

Alignment

The four dimensions share a consistent visual relationship to one another — proximity and evenness, not a shared processing line. Access Denied is deliberately set apart from the dimension group, reachable from more than one of its members, so it is never read as a continuation of any single one of them. Non-Panel Actor Authorization is set apart further still, in a different direction from Access Denied, so the two elements outside the group are never confused with one another.

Relative proportions

All four dimensions share one common size class. Neither Authorization Scope nor Governance Rules may be enlarged beyond what is needed to hold its internal detail at a subordinate type size; at a glance, all four should read as members of the same set, none more prominent than another.

Visual rhythm and balance

Spacing among the four dimensions is even throughout — no gap is longer than another between any two of them, in deliberate contrast to Figure A, which broke its own rhythm at its one dominant anchor. Balance across the whole canvas is asymmetric by design: the outcome layer and the non-panel reasoning domain sit at different distances and different visual weights from the dimension group, so the canvas does not read as mirror-symmetric, which would wrongly suggest they are equivalent, competing branches rather than one shared outcome layer and one entirely separate reasoning domain.

11Reading Order

  1. The reader first takes in the four dimensions as one composed group — same size, same weight — in under five seconds, before reading a single word: "authorization is the combination of four equally necessary dimensions, and none of them outranks the others."
  2. The reader takes in each dimension by name — Role Model, Business Membership, Authorization Scope, Governance Rules — reconstructing them as conditions that jointly hold, not as stages visited in order. Which one the eye happens to notice first is incidental; none is a prerequisite the others wait on.
  3. The reader notices the short internal lists inside Authorization Scope and Governance Rules, understanding these as richness within one dimension, never as further dimensions of their own.
  4. The reader notices that Access Denied is reachable from more than one dimension, understanding "a decision can be turned away by any of three of them, immediately" as a single idea, not three separate endings.
  5. The reader notices the single connection from Role Model to Non-Panel Actor Authorization, understanding that Customer, Bot, and System are resolved by an entirely different reasoning domain the rest of the figure never touches.
  6. Last, the reader reaches Business Operation, the model's sole positive resolution — reached not because a sequence completed, but because every dimension held at once.

A reader who comes away thinking "step 1, step 2, step 3, step 4" has misread the figure, even if the node count is right. A reader who comes away thinking "authorization is the combination of four equally necessary decision dimensions — and non-panel actors are a separate story" has read it correctly.

12Visual Grammar

Shapes

Borders

All four primary-tier nodes share identical border weight — no exceptions, no "slightly heavier" anywhere in that tier. The two outcome nodes carry a visibly lighter border than the primary tier. Non-Panel Actor Authorization carries the lightest border on the canvas.

Typography

ElementSpecification
Primary-tier node titlesSans-serif, medium weight, identical size across all four — no exceptions.
Internal sub-lists (Scope, Governance)Sans-serif, regular weight, smaller than the node title, visibly subordinate — never styled as independent floating labels.
Outcome node labelsSans-serif, medium weight, smaller than primary-tier titles.
Access Denied labelUpper case, reused from Figure A's convention for the same outcome.
Legend and captionsSans-serif, regular weight, the smallest text tier on the canvas.

Spacing

Identical, even spacing between each consecutive pair of primary-tier nodes — this is the figure's primary tool for expressing "these four are equally necessary," in deliberate contrast to Figure A's broken rhythm at its one dominant anchor. The denial lane sits in a consistent, narrow corridor beside the spine; the non-panel branch sits in the widest gap on the canvas.

Accent colours

The single reserved accent colour already established for this Journey is applied identically and lightly to all four primary-tier nodes, as a family marker distinguishing the spine from the outcomes and the parallel track — never to single out one node above the others. No primary-tier node may carry a heavier or more saturated use of the accent than any other. No other node, in any tier, carries colour.

Weight

Visual weight ranking: the four primary-tier nodes (equal to one another) → the two outcome nodes (equal to one another, lighter than the primary tier) → Non-Panel Actor Authorization (lightest on the canvas). No node outside this ranking may be styled to compete with the tier above it.

Negative space

The gap between the primary tier and the denial lane, and the larger gap between the spine and the non-panel branch, are themselves part of the grammar — their relative size communicates relative conceptual distance without requiring a single additional colour, icon, or label.

13Publication Checklist

  • Every node maps to a sentence already present in AUTHORIZATION.md's Figure B section, Governance Rules section, or Role Model section.
  • No node represents an implementation detail — no class, no method, no endpoint, no aggregate.
  • No connector implies chronology, execution order, or request lifecycle.
  • Access Denied is rendered as a single shared outcome, reachable from three points, never as a fourth or fifth sequential stage.
  • Customer, Bot, and System remain visibly outside the platform role hierarchy, connected only to Role Model.
  • Every visual distinction on the canvas has an architectural meaning stated somewhere in this document — nothing is decorative.
  • The figure survives being reproduced in greyscale, with no loss of any distinction described in §12.
  • Colour is never required to understand the figure — shape, size, weight, and spacing carry the meaning first.
  • Shapes carry semantics consistently: rounded rectangle = primary-tier concept, double border = granted outcome, octagon = denied outcome, pill = non-panel mechanism.
  • No decorative elements: no gradients, no drop shadows, no icons, no illustrative imagery.

14Non-Negotiable Constraints

  • Exactly seven nodes: Role Model, Business Membership, Authorization Scope, Governance Rules, Business Operation, Access Denied, Non-Panel Actor Authorization. No node may be added, and no internal sub-list item (the three scope breadths, the four governance rules) may be promoted to a node of its own.
  • Exactly nine connectors, exactly as enumerated in §8. No connector may be added, removed, or repointed.
  • No node among Role Model, Business Membership, Authorization Scope, and Governance Rules may be rendered larger, heavier, or more saturated than any other in that group, under any circumstance, including differences in internal content volume.
  • No pixel coordinates, absolute positioning, or fixed canvas dimensions may govern the rendering — only the relational rules in §10.
  • No new architecture, no new governance rule, and no reinterpretation of AUTHORIZATION.md may be introduced through this figure. If a rendering choice would require inventing a fact not already documented, the rendering choice is wrong, not the documentation.
  • The only class or component name permitted anywhere in the figure is AuthorizationService — already published in AX-4's Related Source Artefacts — and only in prose, never as a node label, and the figure must not require it to be understood.
  • No individual endpoint, business operation instance, or aggregate name (Appointment, BlockedSlot, User, etc.) may appear as a node anywhere in the figure.
  • Business Operation and Access Denied must use the exact shape vocabulary already defined for them in Figure-A-v2.html — a renderer may not invent new shapes for outcomes already established elsewhere in this Journey.
  • The published caption must follow the Architecture Style Guide's caption format, link to ADR-016, and include one explicit sentence distinguishing this figure's conceptual-narrowing axis from Figure A's enforcement-tier axis, so the two are never mistaken for two views of the same thing.

15Rendering Notes

Practical guidance for whoever converts this specification into the final publication asset:

  • If a rendered image still resembles a traditional flowchart, the renderer has misunderstood this blueprint. The rendered figure should immediately communicate "decision model," never "request lifecycle." An ordered column of boxes with arrows running straight down is the single most likely failure mode for this figure, and it must be treated as a rendering defect, not a stylistic variation.
  • Seek a distinct visual identity for this figure, not merely a compliant one. Figure A is memorable because everything visibly converges toward one anchor. Figure B needs an equally strong identity of its own — as a balanced decision model built from a conceptual backbone, surrounding reasoning domains, and grouped decision dimensions — without borrowing Figure A's single-anchor solution and without defaulting to an unstyled column of boxes. The specific compositional technique is the renderer's to find; the requirement is only that the result feels composed, not listed.
  • Treat the four dimensions as a single reusable template — same box, same type scale, same border, same accent — populated with different titles and (for two of them) a short internal list. Do not design each of the four independently; design one template and instantiate it four times.
  • The accent colour used here is the same variable already reserved for Figure A's dominant anchor. Do not introduce a new colour for this figure — reusing the existing accent, applied evenly rather than concentrated, is what signals "same Journey, different emphasis."
  • Reuse Figure A's exact Business Operation and Access Denied shapes without modification. These outcome nodes are shared vocabulary across the whole AX-4 figure set, not new designs.
  • This figure is intended to sit immediately after Figure A in the published document. Its caption should explicitly note the shift from "where" to "how," so a reader moving from one figure to the next understands why the composition changed from a single-anchor hierarchy to an even, four-dimension decision model.
  • If a future rendering pass is tempted to give Authorization Scope or Governance Rules extra visual weight because they contain more text, that temptation should be resisted — see §9's explicit warning. The correct response to "this node has more to say" is smaller internal type, not a bigger box.
  • This document supersedes no architecture and introduces none. Any question about what a node means should be answered by re-reading AUTHORIZATION.md's Figure B section and Governance Rules section, not by inferring further detail from this blueprint.
