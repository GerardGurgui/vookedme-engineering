Architecture Experience Journey — AX-4 — Authorization Architecture

Figure A — Authorization Enforcement Architecture

Engineering blueprint, Version 2 · Source of truth: docs/architecture/AUTHORIZATION.md (frozen) · Baseline: Figure-A.html (v1)

This is an editorial redesign, not an architectural revision. Every fact in this document is identical in meaning to Version 1 and to the frozen AUTHORIZATION.md specification. What changes here is how the blueprint is expressed — architecturally rather than as coordinates, and by responsibility rather than by execution sequence.

01Engineering Question

Where in the request lifecycle is a business-authorization decision actually made, and by what mechanism?

02Purpose

This figure teaches a reader that business authorization is not a single check — it is a small number of responsibility tiers of increasing precision, and one of those tiers carries almost all of the real weight. Two tiers are coarse and cover only a small, fixed set of platform-wide cases. One tier — a single dedicated component — is where the overwhelming majority of business, ownership, and self-scoped decisions are actually resolved. The figure exists to make that imbalance of responsibility unmistakable at a glance.

This figure does not explain how identity is established, how credentials are issued, or how the request-security pipeline is ordered. Those questions belong to AX-5 — Security Architecture. This figure begins at the point where identity is already known.

03Visual Philosophy

This figure explains WHERE authorization decisions are made — not HOW a request executes internally. It is a responsibility map, not a sequence diagram. A reader should come away understanding which part of the system owns which category of decision. Whether one tier happens to run before another in a given millisecond is incidental information, not the figure's subject.
Chronology is secondary. Architectural responsibility is primary. Where the figure must show order (because a decision genuinely narrows in scope as it passes through the tiers), that order is expressed as a hierarchy of precision — coarse to fine — not as a countdown of execution steps. Nothing in this figure should read as "step 1, step 2, step 3." Everything should read as "this tier owns a little of the decision, this tier owns a little more, this tier owns almost all of it."
Every visual decision reinforces responsibility, never sequence. Size, weight, and spacing are allocated according to how much of the authorization question each element resolves — never according to when it happens to act. This is the single test for every layout, colour, and typography choice in the sections that follow: does it communicate how much responsibility this element holds?

Connector Philosophy

  • Nodes dominate edges. A reader should be able to understand the entire architecture from the nodes alone, with the connectors closed off from view.
  • Relationships never dominate components. No connector may be styled — in weight, colour, or length — so that it competes with a node for attention.
  • Connectors explain; nodes teach. A connector's only job is to clarify which relationship links two already-understood nodes. It introduces no new concept of its own.
  • No connector should visually attract attention before the nodes. This applies even to the connectors touching the primary anchor — proximity to the most important node does not entitle a connector to borrow its visual weight.

05Layout Blueprint

This section describes relationships and proportions, not coordinates. Nothing here should be read as a pixel value or a fixed measurement — a renderer should interpret it as a set of relative rules to satisfy, in whatever concrete layout it produces.

Primary vertical axis

The spine of the figure. It expresses a hierarchy of increasing decision precision, not a sequence of execution steps. A position's place on the axis answers "how much of the authorization question does this tier resolve," never "what happens at time T." Five elements share this axis: the entry marker, the two coarse tiers, the primary anchor, and the granted outcome.

Anchor points

Business Authorization is the structural centre of gravity for the entire figure — not necessarily its geometric midpoint, but the point every other element's size, weight, and spacing is chosen relative to. No element's proportions should be decided by looking at the canvas as a whole; they should be decided by looking at Business Authorization and asking "how much smaller and quieter should this be, by comparison."

Secondary branch

A region set apart from the primary axis, reserved for Access Denied — the single shared negative outcome. Its separation from the axis is itself meaningful and load-bearing: if this region were ever placed inline with the primary axis, a reader would misread the outcome as a fourth tier rather than a shared exit available to all three tiers. The branch must remain visually and spatially distinct from the axis at every stage of rendering.

Negative space

Whitespace is the primary technique — alongside size — used to establish Business Authorization's dominance without leaning on colour. The clearance surrounding Business Authorization must exceed the clearance surrounding any other element, proportionally and unmistakably, not subtly.

Visual rhythm and reading cadence

The spacing between the entry marker, Platform Authorization, and Endpoint Authorization should feel like an even, steady cadence — a reader moves past them without pausing. That cadence deliberately breaks at Business Authorization: the pause before and after it should be longer than any other gap on the axis, signalling "slow down, this is where the figure's meaning lives." The cadence resumes evenly between Business Authorization and Business Operation.

Alignment rules

  • The entry marker, both coarse tiers, Business Authorization, and Business Operation share one common centreline. This is the primary axis.
  • Access Denied is deliberately off this centreline. It must never share the centreline, regardless of how the surrounding layout is otherwise adjusted.
  • Business Operation remains on the centreline because it is the direct continuation of the responsibility chain when a decision is granted — it is an outcome of the axis, not a branch from it.

Proportional sizing

Business Authorization must be unmistakably larger than every other element — not by a subtle margin, but enough that scale alone communicates its role before any label is read. The entry marker, the two coarse tiers, and the two outcome nodes share one common, smaller scale among themselves; none of them should be sized to suggest it is more important than the others.

06Every Visual Node

Nodes are grouped into three categories, matching the responsibility model: an entry marker, the three authorization tiers, and the two terminal outcomes. The two outcomes are described separately from the tiers deliberately — they are not a fourth and fifth tier, they are what a tier's decision resolves into.

Node Taxonomy — Naming Rationale

Two node names were revised from Version 1 for architectural clarity. No meaning changed; only the label. Both revisions remain fully traceable to the frozen specification.

v1 namev2 nameGrounding in AUTHORIZATION.md
Route-Level CheckPlatform Authorization"a coarse route-level check that establishes whether the request is authenticated at all and gates a small number of platform-wide operations by role"
Declarative Endpoint CheckEndpoint Authorization"a declarative per-endpoint check that narrows a handful of operations to a single global role"
Dedicated Authorization ComponentBusiness Authorization"a dedicated authorization component that resolves every business-, ownership-, and self-scoped decision"

The rationale for each rename: "Route-Level" and "Declarative Endpoint Check" describe how a check is implemented (as an HTTP route rule, as a declarative annotation) — implementation-flavoured language this figure should avoid per the Visual Philosophy above. "Platform Authorization," "Endpoint Authorization," and "Business Authorization" describe what each tier is responsible for, which is this figure's actual subject.

Connectors exist only to show which tiers hand an unresolved question to which other tier, and which tier's decision resolves into which outcome. No connector introduces a concept not already present in the two nodes it joins.

Connector styling rules

  • Every connector, without exception, is styled more quietly than the quietest node — including the connectors touching Business Authorization. Proximity to the primary anchor does not raise a connector's visual rank.
  • Solid vs. dashed is the only stylistic distinction between the two connector families. Neither family uses colour, and neither uses a label.
  • C1–C4 form a single unbroken line down the primary axis; no other connector may cross it.
  • C5, C6, and C7 converge on Access Denied from three distinct points, without overlapping one another or passing through any node other than their own two endpoints.

08Colour Language

Colour is the least important tool available for expressing this figure's hierarchy, and is used last, after shape, size, weight, and spacing have already done the work. If every colour in this figure were removed and rendered in greyscale, the hierarchy — one dominant tier, everything else recessive, one shared negative outcome — must still be fully legible.

Accent (Business Authorization border, and optionally a very light fill)Least important of five signals (after size, weight, spacing, and shape)Reinforces — does not establish — the dominance already communicated by scale and whitespace.

10Legend

Solid line — a tier defers an unresolved question to the next tier, or a granted decision reaches the protected operation.

Dashed line — a tier resolves directly into the shared denied outcome.

Heaviest border, largest size — the primary anchor; owns nearly all authorization responsibility.

Octagon — shared denied outcome, not a processing tier.

Double border — the protected business operation; guarded, not guarding.

Pill — the entry marker; a boundary, not a decision-maker.

11Reading Order

Reading order here is governed by visual weight, not by physical position on the axis. This is a deliberate departure from a literal top-to-bottom trace.

  1. The eye is drawn first to Business Authorization — its scale and clearance should command attention before any deliberate scanning begins. This establishes the figure's main takeaway immediately: one component holds nearly all of the responsibility.
  2. Only afterward does the reader trace the axis for context — noticing Incoming Request at the top, and Platform Authorization and Endpoint Authorization as smaller, evenly-paced elements above the anchor, understood as narrowing tiers rather than as steps in a countdown.
  3. The reader then follows the chain downward to Business Operation, understanding it as what a granted decision resolves into.
  4. Last, the reader notices the three dashed lines reaching Access Denied off to the side, understanding it as a shared exit available to any tier — not a fourth step reached only after the other three.

A reader who comes away thinking "there are four sequential steps" has misread the figure. A reader who comes away thinking "one component does almost all of the work, and two lightweight gates handle the rest" has read it correctly.

12Accessibility

  • No information in this figure depends on colour perception alone. Business Authorization is distinguished by size, border weight, whitespace, and label size, with colour contributing last. Access Denied is distinguished by shape (octagon), off-axis position, and an explicit upper-case label, with colour contributing last.
  • The distinction between "defer to the next tier" and "resolve into denial" is carried by line style (solid vs. dashed), legible in greyscale.
  • The distinction between an outcome node and a responsibility-holding tier is carried by shape (double border vs. plain rectangle vs. octagon vs. pill), not by colour.
  • If this figure is ever reproduced in greyscale or print, every distinction described above must remain fully legible without exception.

13Non-Negotiable Constraints — Publication Checklist

  • Exactly six nodes: Incoming Request, Platform Authorization, Endpoint Authorization, Business Authorization, Business Operation, Access Denied. No additional node.
  • Exactly seven connectors (four chain, three outcome). No additional connector.
  • Business Authorization is the only semantic focal point. No second point of emphasis is permitted anywhere on the canvas.
  • Dominance is established primarily through size, weight, and spacing. Colour is the least important signal and must remain removable without loss of legibility (see Accessibility).
  • Access Denied must never be rendered inline with the primary axis, and must never be styled to resemble a fourth processing tier.
  • No connector may be styled with greater visual weight than the least prominent node on the canvas.
  • No connector carries a text label.
  • The only class or component name appearing anywhere in the figure is AuthorizationService — already published in AX-4's Related Source Artefacts. No other class name, package name, method name, file path, or line number may appear.
  • Platform Authorization and Endpoint Authorization must remain generic — no specific role name may be attached to either tier. The frozen specification deliberately does not name which role gates these tiers.
  • The figure must not depict, label, or hint at the legacy-authorization-check consolidation note from the surrounding prose in AUTHORIZATION.md. That note is Level B content for the accompanying text, never for the figure itself.
  • No gradients, no drop shadows, no decorative icons, no photographic or illustrative imagery.
  • No absolute coordinates, fixed canvas dimensions, or pixel measurements may govern the final rendering — only the relational rules in the Layout Blueprint.
  • Every element in this specification maps to a sentence already present in the frozen AUTHORIZATION.md Figure A description, or to the Node Taxonomy Rationale table above. Nothing in this blueprint may be traced to a source not already approved for publication.
  • The published caption must follow the Architecture Style Guide's caption format exactly, and must link only to ADR-016.
